HERO INTRO:
# Mornington Peninsula: A Family Holiday Destination

The Mornington Peninsula offers everything families need for a proper beach holiday without the fuss of travelling too far from Melbourne. You've got sheltered beaches perfect for younger kids at places like Rosebud and Dromana, plus rockier spots at Portsea for rock pooling and exploring. The natural landscape is genuinely impressive – think coastal heathland, native bushland trails, and plenty of space for children to burn off energy. Key attractions like the Mornington Peninsula Hot Springs, wildlife parks, and casual beachside adventure golf keep kids entertained without draining the budget, while parents can relax knowing there's good infrastructure and plenty of family-friendly cafes dotted along the foreshore.

Getting accommodation sorted is straightforward with several reliable caravan parks in the area including BIG4 Mornington Peninsula Holiday Park near Frankston, Peninsula Holiday Park, Capel Caravan Park, Amberlee Family Holidays, and Kanasta Caravan Park – all offering the basics families need with on-site facilities and easy access to beaches and attractions. What makes the Peninsula genuinely good for families is the variety; whether you're after a quiet day at the beach, hiking through nature reserves, or checking out local markets and wineries (the grown-ups' bonus), there's something for everyone. It's close enough for long weekends but far enough away to feel properly removed from the city grind.

LOCAL KNOWLEDGE:
# Mornington Peninsula Family Guide

The best time to visit is December through February when the weather's warm and the beaches are proper swimming territory – though it'll be packed and pricey. If you've got flexibility, October or April are gold: the weather's still beautiful, crowds thin out, and you won't need to remortgage for accommodation. School holidays are a nightmare for traffic, especially getting down the Nepean Highway on Friday arvo – leave early or stay overnight somewhere on the way. Winter's quieter but you'll need to pick your days weather-wise, though the penguin parade at Phillip Island is still worth the trip if it's clear.

Grab a cafe culture breakfast at Mornington township rather than the beachside spots – you'll actually get a table and decent coffee without waiting an hour. The wineries aren't really kid-friendly despite what the brochures suggest, so if you want a wine stop, book ahead and plan proper childcare because the little ones get bored fast. Pack layers even in summer because the wind whips up quick, and the southern beaches are cold and rough – stick to Portsea, Sorrento, or Flinders for safer swimming. The one thing locals wish visitors knew: the roads are narrow and winding, so don't speed, and watch for cyclists who seem to think they own the joint.

What families actually love doing is the short walks to viewpoints, the penguin colony at sunset (book tickets weeks ahead), rock pooling at Blairgowrie, and grabbing fish and chips to eat on the grass. Pro tip: visit the beaches on the bay side (like Sorrento Beach) if you've got young kids – they're calmer and warmer than the ocean-facing stretches. The secret weapon is visiting in shoulder season when you get the best of everything without the tourist madness and inflated prices.

DESTINATION SUMMARY:
The Mornington Peninsula's holiday park scene operates in a uniquely privileged position – within an hour of Melbourne yet feeling genuinely remote. The five main parks dotting the region cater to distinct preferences: BIG4 Mornington Peninsula near Frankston anchors the northern gateway with resort-style facilities, while Peninsula Holiday Park and Capel Caravan Park sit closer to the Peninsula's heart, offering more intimate settings. Amberlee Family Holidays and Kanasta appeal to those seeking quieter, less commercialised experiences. What separates these parks from anywhere else along Victoria's coast is the geography itself – you've got the bay side with calmer waters perfect for young swimmers, the ocean side with serious surf breaks at Gunnamatta and Winki Pop, and pockets of native bush thick enough to feel properly remote despite suburban sprawl creeping closer each year. Most parks sit within 20 minutes of multiple beaches, wineries, and the Mornington Peninsula National Park's walking trails.

The visitor profile has shifted noticeably over the past decade. School holidays – particularly Christmas-New Year and winter – absolutely pack the parks, with families booking 12 months ahead for decent sites. But you'll also find experienced caravaners and motorhomers who've been coming for 15-20 years, treating certain parks like second homes. They know exactly which sites catch afternoon shade, which ones back onto the quietest roads, and which parks have upgraded their amenities without losing character. Solo travellers and couples without kids increasingly favour the smaller parks – Capel and Kanasta especially – where you're not surrounded by screaming children at 7am.
