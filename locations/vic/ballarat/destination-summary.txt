Ballarat's holiday park scene has quietly become a go-to for Victorian caravanners and families who've figured out what locals have known for years – the town sits in genuinely interesting country without requiring a coastal drive. The parks here aren't competing on beach access; they're drawing repeat visitors because of what's actually within reach: the Yarrowee River corridor for walking, Lake Wendouree for water activities, and the sprawling gardens and heritage sites that make Ballarat different from your standard regional stop. The BIG4 Windmill sits on substantial grounds that feel properly removed from town bustle, while the NRMA and Eureka Stockade parks are more centrally positioned for those who want walkable access to the historic precinct. Shady Acres and the Kryal Castle property offer different vibes – one's genuinely bush-oriented, the other leaning into family attractions – which is why you'll find the same caravan regulars rotating between them depending on what they're after that trip.

The visitor demographic has shifted noticeably over the past decade. You've still got your traditional grey nomads working through their park checklist, but increasingly these parks are filling with families from Melbourne using Ballarat as a proper destination rather than an overnight stop. School holidays see genuine capacity pressure – the parks aren't pretending to have beachfront allure, but they're filling anyway because families know they can do the Gold Museum, wander Sovereign Hill, spend a day at the lake, and actually have time to breathe. Motorhome and caravan sites dominate, though the parks have progressively added cabin accommodation that's bringing in people who wouldn't normally tow. The repeat visitor rate here is genuinely high – families book the same park, same site sometimes, year after year, which tells you something about what they're finding.

What keeps people coming back to Ballarat parks specifically is the combination of genuine quietness and proper things to do. The river access and garden walks aren't novelties – they're actual reasons to spend a week rather than a night. Unlike truly remote bush parks, you've got decent shopping, real restaurants, and services without losing that sense of being properly away. The parks themselves have invested in facilities that work for extended stays: proper laundries, well-maintained grounds, reliable amenities. It's the kind of place where families settle in for ten days during winter school holidays, discover they actually prefer it to coastal destinations, and start planning next year's trip before they even pack up. Ballarat works because it's honest about what it is – a substantial inland town with good bones and genuine appeal – rather than trying to be somewhere it's not.
