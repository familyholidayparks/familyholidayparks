Wilsons Promontory's holiday park scene is built around one undeniable fact: location. The three main parks—Toora Tourist Park, BIG4 Wilsons Prom Holiday Park, and Tidal River Campground—sit within spitting distance of Victoria's most dramatic coastal landscape, where granite peaks drop straight into the ocean and walking trails lead to secluded beaches you can't reach any other way. What makes this different from other coastal park destinations is the sheer wildness on your doorstep. You're not just near a beach; you're camped at the gateway to a national park that feels genuinely remote, even though it's only three hours from Melbourne. BIG4 and Toora cater to the caravan and motorhome crowd with proper facilities and powered sites, while Tidal River operates as the more rustic, bush-camp alternative within the national park itself, where you're trading convenience for proximity to some of Australia's best coastal hiking.

The people who book these parks year after year tend to fall into two camps: families with school-age kids who've worked out that the Prom delivers better value than crowded theme parks, and serious caravanners who've moved beyond generic holiday parks and want genuine natural experiences. School holidays—particularly summer and autumn—fill these parks completely, sometimes months in advance. Repeat visitors know the rhythm: book early for Christmas and winter breaks, plan mid-week trips in shoulder seasons, and always pack layers because the weather changes fast down here. They come for the beaches at Squeaky and Oberon Bay, the granite tors that dominate the skyline, and the walking tracks that genuinely challenge you. Families return because kids can actually burn energy here—proper all-day hikes, rock scrambling, and beach exploration—rather than being managed in an entertainment precinct.

What keeps people coming back is the combination of accessibility and authenticity. You can pull up in a caravan with full creature comforts, but within ten minutes you're hiking through eucalypt forest toward ocean views that don't look like anything else in Victoria. Tidal River fills fastest because campers understand they're trading power hookups for the rare experience of camping within a national park that actually feels protected and wild. The river and creek systems around Toora offer alternate attractions for those who want something less exposed than the coast, though honestly most people are here for the promontory itself—the headland walks, the offshore islands visible from camp, and the sense that you're at the southern edge of something significant. These parks work because they've stayed relatively unspoiled and because the natural attractions are genuinely world-class, not just adequate. Once families experience the Prom properly, the other holiday parks start feeling thin.
