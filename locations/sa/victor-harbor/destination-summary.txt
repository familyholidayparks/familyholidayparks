Victor Harbor's holiday park scene is dominated by the NRMA Victor Harbor Beachfront Holiday Park, which essentially is the main game in town for powered accommodation. The park's position on the foreshore gives direct beach access—you can walk straight onto the sand from your van or cabin, which is the real drawcard here. The Fleurieu Peninsula's geography means the park sits in a sheltered spot with views across the bay, and the nearby Granite Island connected by causeway adds another layer of appeal. Unlike parks scattered inland, everything about this location is oriented around what people actually come here for: the water, the southern ocean views, and easy access to coastal walks. The park itself caters to the full range of visitors—caravan and motorhome sites with varying levels of power hookup, plus cabin accommodation for those without their own vehicles—so you'll see everything from long-term grey nomads to families arriving in school holidays.

The booking patterns here follow the predictable South Australian school holiday peaks pretty heavily, particularly the summer break and winter holidays. Repeat visitors are genuinely common; families who've been coming for a decade or more treat it almost like a private retreat. This happens because Victor Harbor delivers something reliable that doesn't require endless exploring—the beach is right there, the rock pools and penguin colony on Granite Island keep kids occupied, and there's enough cafes and fish and chips shops within walking distance that you're not stuck cooking every night. The river access nearby (the Inman River) gives a calmer alternative to ocean swimming, and the surrounding bush walking is solid without being overwhelming. Parents know what they're getting, which removes the stress of holiday planning.

What keeps families returning year after year is genuinely about the straightforward nature of the place. It's not trying to be a flashy resort destination—there's no theme parks or forced entertainment. Instead, it's a proper coastal holiday where kids can be kids: collecting shells, spotting little penguins at dusk on Granite Island, playing in rock pools, and actually getting tired out by simple stuff. The town itself has developed enough infrastructure that a week here doesn't feel cramped, but it's still quiet enough that you're not fighting crowds. Caravaners and motorhomers appreciate that the park is professionally run, the facilities are maintained, and it's a logical stop between Adelaide and other Fleurieu destinations. School holiday demand does spike, so booking ahead is essential during January, July, and the shorter breaks, but the park's relatively straightforward appeal means it maintains steady visitation throughout the year from travellers who understand what they're coming for.
