# Visiting Robe with the Family

The best time to hit up Robe is late November through early February when the weather's warm enough for proper beach days, though you'll want to book accommodation well ahead as school holidays get rammed. If you can swing a shoulder season visit in March or April, you'll dodge the crowds and still get decent weather. Avoid June to August unless you're keen on cold coastal winds – the beaches aren't really swimmable then. Pro tip: the Great Southern Touring Route works brilliantly if you're combining Robe with other towns, and timing it for when the salmon are running (usually March-April) makes for a seriously fun family outing if anyone wants to have a go at fishing.

The main drag is Mundy Street where you'll find the shops, pubs, and decent cafes – grab coffee at one of the spots there rather than relying on the holiday parks. The beaches are split between Robe Beach (safer for littlies, patrolled in summer), Hummock Beach (rockier, good for exploring pools), and Guichen Bay if you want something quieter. Keep an eye on the tides if you're doing beach exploring – they swing pretty hard here and can cut off access to certain spots. The town fills up massively during school holidays so if you're after a quieter, more authentic experience, skip those periods. Accommodation books out fast, especially decent holiday homes, so lock it in early. Watch out for the local stingers in summer and always check conditions before swimming – the Southern Ocean doesn't stuff around.

What locals absolutely love showing their kids is the lighthouse climb (great views, bit of a workout), exploring the rock pools at low tide, and the Robe Woollen Mills if the weather's ordinary. The caravan park is solid and family-friendly, though pricey during peak season. Honestly, the real gem is just the laid-back vibe – it's not overhyped like some coastal towns, so families can actually relax. Grab fish and chips, walk along the jetty, let the kids climb rocks, and you've got a ripper of a few days. One last thing: download offline maps before you go because mobile reception can be patchy in spots, and fill up fuel before heading further inland.
