Mandurah's holiday parks sit in a sweet spot between the Indian Ocean and the Peel-Harvey Estuary, which fundamentally shapes what you get here compared to parks further north or south. The two main parks—Mandurah Caravan and Tourist Park and Mandurah Coastal Holiday Park—both leverage this dual-water-body advantage, though differently. The Caravan and Tourist Park is closer to town and the estuary foreshore, making it popular with older caravanners and families who want walkable access to the main strip without constantly moving their rig. The Coastal park sits closer to the actual beach at Silver Sands, which pulls in the motorhome crowd and families keen on ocean swimming over calmer estuary paddling. You won't find sprawling bush parks here like you do inland—Mandurah's appeal is water access, not isolation.

The demographic at these parks shifts noticeably with the school calendar. During term time, you'll see mostly retirees in permanent or semi-permanent vans, couples doing lap work, and the occasional young family taking a week off-season. Come school holidays—particularly the two-week winter and summer breaks—both parks fill to capacity with families, often the same families year after year. Parents return because there's genuine variety without having to pack up and move: kids can switch between safe estuary paddling, proper swimming at the beach, and quieter foreshore walks depending on the day and tide. The river's calm waters mean younger kids can actually do water activities their parents feel comfortable with, which you don't get at Cottesloe or the Southern Ocean beaches. Repeat visitors book 12 months ahead, and some families have been coming for twenty-odd years.

What keeps people coming back isn't fancy resort amenities—these are solid working parks, not luxury destinations. It's that Mandurah as a town has matured into something liveable without being overdeveloped. The estuary's protected status means the water quality and wildlife remain consistent year to year. Families fish off the jetties, spot dolphins regularly during winter, and do the Mandurah Boardwalk without the chaos of Perth beaches. The parks themselves offer reliable facilities, reasonable rates, and proximity to decent takeaway, pubs, and produce. For caravanners, the locations tick boxes—power and water sites, dump points, good roads for towing in and out. There's a reason the same vans show up in the same parks at the same time each year: Mandurah delivers predictably on what it promises, which in caravan culture is worth more than novelty.
