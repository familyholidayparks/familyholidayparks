Bundaberg's holiday park scene thrives on its unique position straddling coastal and riverside experiences, something you won't find in other Queensland destinations quite the same way. The town sits where the Burnett River meets the ocean, and the parks here are deliberately positioned to take advantage of both environments. Burnett Heads Holiday Park sits right at the river mouth with direct beach access, while AAOK Riverdale Caravan Park capitalises on the slower, more sheltered river life upstream. Moore Park Beach Holiday Park offers something different again—beachfront positioning with a quieter vibe than the main tourist beaches further south. This variety means different types of travellers get what they're after without compromise, whether that's anglers after the tidal flows, families wanting safe swimming, or grey nomads simply looking for a peaceful base with good facilities.

The parks draw a predictable but dedicated crowd: caravanners and motorhome owners make up the backbone of visitors, particularly during autumn and winter when they're escaping southern weather. School holidays pack these parks to capacity—you'll struggle to find a site during July and December if you haven't booked months ahead. But what's notable about Bundaberg is the volume of repeat visitors, particularly families who book the same park at the same time every year. They return because the parks offer genuine river and beach access without the chaos of the Gold Coast or Sunshine Coast, and the local area delivers activities beyond just caravan park lounging—the Mon Repos turtle rookery, the Bundaberg Rum Distillery, reasonable fishing opportunities, and low-key beach towns like Moore Park and Burnett Heads proper.

The physical infrastructure across these five parks ranges from basic to mid-range resort-style facilities, which suits the market. You've got powered sites with full hookups, self-contained cabins for families who don't own caravans, and bush-style unpowered sites for traditionalists. The parks themselves aren't flashy—they're well-maintained working parks rather than playground destinations—and that's exactly why people keep coming back. Families appreciate that kids can ride bikes between sites safely, that there's direct river or beach access for morning swims, and that you're not paying resort prices for basic amenities. For caravanners, the accessibility to water for fishing or simply launching a tinny makes these parks practical bases rather than just overnight stops. Bundaberg has carved out a niche as the thoughtful alternative to the more commercialised caravan destinations further up the coast.
