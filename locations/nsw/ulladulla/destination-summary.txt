Ulladulla's holiday park scene sits at a sweet spot on the South Coast—close enough to Sydney for long weekends, far enough to feel genuinely removed. What sets these parks apart is the sheer variety of water access within a tight radius. BIG4 Ulladulla Beachside sits right on the sand, giving you that walk-straight-onto-the-beach setup families crave. Holiday Haven and Discovery Parks at Burrill Lake tap into a completely different vibe—you're on a massive freshwater lake system where the water's calmer, better for kids learning to kayak or paddleboard, and the birdlife keeps older visitors occupied for hours. The Ingenia and Holiday Haven properties around town offer the in-between option for people who want the South Coast experience without the salt air eating their van. Most parks blend open-plan caravan areas with self-contained cabins, so whether you're rolling up in a 25-footer or booking a week in a villa, there's infrastructure built for repeat stays rather than churn.

The clientele tells you everything. You'll see grey nomads who winter here for three months straight, families who've booked the same two weeks every January for a decade, and local Sydney caravanners who've worked out that a Friday-to-Sunday blitz to Ulladulla costs less in petrol and accommodation than the Hawkesbury spots everyone else fights for. School holidays absolutely pack these parks—book by May if you want July, by August if you want December. But the shoulder seasons are where the locals know the real value sits. September and April are genuinely pleasant, the parks are maybe 60 percent full, and you can actually grab a site without pre-booking six months out. The repeat visitors aren't accident—families come back because their kids remember where the rock pools are, they've got a favourite spot in the caravan park, and the beach doesn't require a two-hour drive home when the weather turns.

What keeps people returning year after year is the practical layup: Ulladulla Beach and Mollymook are genuinely good swimming and fishing spots, Burrill Lake gives you bushwalking through the Budderoo plateau without leaving the park boundaries, and the town itself has evolved beyond the old fishing village stereotype without becoming Gold Coast-tacky. The parks sit between proper coastal attractions—dolphin-spotting cruises, rock pool walks at Narrawallee, the hinterland drives toward Kangaroo Valley—and genuine downtime. Most families will spend two days doing stuff, then three days where the kids just want to be in the park's jumping pillow or playing at the foreshore reserve. The parks get this. They're not trying to be theme parks or five-star resorts; they're just well-maintained bases where the main attraction—the water, the weather, the space to breathe—is literally outside your door.
